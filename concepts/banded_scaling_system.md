This model is not mainly a drifting prime score. It is a banded scaling system, where each divisor family should follow its own stable track.

What changes is that the big problem is not “find the right cutoff,” but “see the hidden tracks the cutoff is slicing through.”

The non-obvious part is that larger ranges should make the structure cleaner at the same time that one fixed cutoff gets worse.

That means the repo’s two strange facts, stronger average separation and collapsing prime recall, are not in conflict; they are the same effect seen two different ways.

You would not have predicted from the current framing that the inverse problem could get easier as the range grows, but one clean track should let you estimate the load setting from its tilt.

The concrete pattern to expect is this: on a chart that uses equal percentage steps on both axes, primes, prime squares, semiprimes, and richer composites should split into separate near-straight bands.

If those bands do not appear, or if their tilt does not stay stable inside each divisor family, this idea is wrong.
